Rhetoric – Figures of Substitution

Leave a reply

Figures of Substitution

Substitution in rhetoric is when images, or elements of images, are substituted with another image or element. Devices such as metaphor are one of the most commonly used figures of substitution in advertising, with substitution used to create interest and diversity to topics , products or services that may be considered saturated or contentious.

Identical Substitution Identitical substitution in rhetoric is when an object or element is substituted with a directly linked, or like for like comparison. Take the following examples for instance:

Advertisement for LG 3D Television Set – In this example, we can see that the perceived TV image has been directly replaced by a three-dimensionalinterpretation. By doing this, the author is displaying the 3D qualities of its television set.

Advertisement for Samsung 3D Television Set – In this example, we can see that the television image has this time been replaced by a capture of teh background mountain range. By associating the television image with the background, the author communicates that there product displays such a realistic and vivid image that it could be easily mistaken for the real thing.

Substitution of Similar Elements Substitution of Similar Elements in rhetoric is when an onject or element is substituted, or associated with, a different object or element, but a connection is communicated between the two. The use of metaphor in this situation is predominant in building links and connections between objects.  Take the following examples for instance:

Advertisement for Recipeace -In this example we can see that the grenade on the top half of the advertisement has been substituted with a similarly shaped artichoke. The connection expressed between the two objects is that one, the grendae, represents war, whilst the other, the artichoke, represent peace and in turn Recipeace and their bid to join conflicting people through food.

Advertisement for Heinz Tomato Ketchup – In this example, we can see that the form of an expensive necklace has been substituted with that of chips with tomato ketchup ‘jewels’. In doing this, the author is communicating that Heinz Tomato Ketchup is a grandiose embellishment to what would otherwise be a plain, ordinary product.

Substitution of Different Elements Substitution of Different Elements in rhetoric is when an object or element is replaced by a completely different element and is broken down into two segments; metonymy and synecdoche . Metonymy is when an associated detail is replaces the whole, whilst synecdoche is when a part replaces the whole. Take the following examples for instance:

Advertisement for Thai Express – In this example, we can see that an associated aspect in the form of steaming running shoes, replaces the wider image of a person delivering Thai Express in speedy fashion. In doing this, the author communicates the speed of delivery by focusing on the smoke created by movement from the shoes.

Advertisement for Vanish stain remover – In this example, the vinegar bottle stands in for visage of a stain. In doing this, the author communicates its message in a visually diverse way.

Substitution of Opposing Elements Substitution of Opposing Elements in rhetoric is divided into three segments; periphrasis , euphemism and antomasia . Periphrasis is when is when an image says something in a vague way, euphemism is when an image says something in an understated way and antomasia is when an image uses words as a pun. Take the following examples for instance:

Advertisement for Nirvana’s ‘Come as You Are’ – In this example, we can see the use of periphrasis in the image of Nirvana lead singer Kurt Cobain (bottom left) super imposed into a scene of American life. The author uses this image of Cobain, looking slightly uncomfortable eating his ice cream, to say in a roundabout way that you need not try to conform to others in your environment, indeed you should ‘come as you are’.

Advertisement for McDonalds Restaurants – In this image, we can see the use of euphemism evident. The author does not need to picture the hamburger described as ‘the worlds most famous’, it simply needs to imply its presence by using the famous colours associated with the global brand of McDonalds. In doing this, the statement becomes somewhat understated whilst also carrying some weight.

Advertisement for Volkswagen – In this example, we can see the use of antomasia evident. The epithet , or phrase expressing a quality of, in this instance is the word ‘lemon’. Lemon describes the compactness of the VW Beetle. The car does not look like a lemon but the qualities associated in size are expressed through the link between word and image.

False Homology

False Homology in rhetoric is the art of puns, or plays on words, in advertising. Take the following examples for instance:

Advertising campaign for Morrisons Supermarket – In the above for images, we can see that this advertising campaign uses the names of famous London roads as visual puns, using the image of an associated food. This play on words creates a playful and engaging visual for the reader to consume.

Advertising campaign for Absolut Vodka – In the above two images, we can see that Absolut Vodka use puns both visually and typographically to link their product to well known popular culture. Linking Absolut to the musical Wicked or the swimming pools so famous in Los Angeles is a playful way of associating the product with the experiences of a wide range of people.

Logo for C’est Cheese – In this example, we can see the use of false homology in a number of ways – c’est cheese is a hint to the phrase ‘say cheese’ through its similar sounding pronunciation, whilst also playing on the well known French term ‘c’est chic’ or ‘it is stylish’.
